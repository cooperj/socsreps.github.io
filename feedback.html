SoCS FEEDBACK

We Need Your Feedback

One of the most important ways SoCS identifies areas that can be improved is by receiving open and honest feedback from its student population throughout the year. We need your help to tell us what areas can be improved or if you think something has been done well (so we can do more of it). No concern or idea is too small.

This Google Forms facility is a shared service that all university school reps have access to. This means that all responses can be read by any school rep across the university and by anyone with an '@lincolnsu.com' email. If you are not happy with this, you can contact any one of us through the email links on our Contact Us page. All emails are received in the strictest of confidence.

NOTE: All feedback is anonymous. You do not need to complete the full form to submit feedback.

All feedback is directly submitted once the submission button is pressed. A 'submitted' response will then show to the user. All submissions are stored and are reviewed almost immediately as every submission triggers an email to the SoCS School Rep. As an example, this question was received as 18:45 and this response is being written at 18:49 and pushed to GitHub at 19:02.

In rare instances some responses don't contain parsable questions but just general feedback which is passed across to the relevant persons, such as module co-coordinators/leads as well as The Director of Teaching & Learning during formal meetings. Because the forms are anonymous there is no way to know who posted the feedback to acknowledge that their comments were passed forwards. We do try were ever possible to try and parse comments into suitable questions for a response so we can add them to the Feedback section for everyone to see. This can be seen from previous questions where they have been broken down into several sections.

Thank you for raising this important topic. Feedback has been gathered from the Module lead (Dr Mark Doughty) which is below. I, as SoCS School Rep, have also added some context to help address some key points raised which are below.

Feedback by Project Module Lead

The Project allocations this year were an incremental step forwards from last year - when random allocation was made of student to academics, with no themes identified. This year we introduced the themes identification to better match students and academics. We don't introduce the theme selection until late in the summer/semester A start as there are a number of students who come to us late to study the third year (direct entrants from partner colleges, overseas students, etc). It has been felt for a while, that a delay in making project allocations would put all students on a more level playing field rather than allocating project supervisors during the previous year - meaning the students which enrol late would have a much reduced set of themes/supervisors to choose from.

There is no 'standard' approach to projects and project allocations throughout the University. Each School/programme is free to decide how to best do this given their circumstances, the type of work which might be undertaken, the number of student/staff, etc. Admittedly we could probably do this better within our own constraints, which is why we look at project allocation methods and scheduling each year - we make incremental changes, if needed, to the project operation each year. It is a large undertaking which we try to improve each year, and we will take this feedback on board when we look at the project for next year.

Response by Garry

Project Selection - The themes provided were only guides to help with project selection. Students were able to forward a personal project as long as it fit the criteria. The 3rd year project has been a key module for many years and it has been an expectation that students think about their dissertation project even from the beginning of year 2. There was also the opportunity this year to pre-select a project focus and potential supervisor via the UVROS programme in July of this year.

20% assignment weighting - For clarification the first assessment for the Project module is 20% and the total project module itself has a CAT score of 45 points (1 x module has a CAT score of 15 points). So as is rightly pointed out the project is worth 3 modules worth of CAT points, however, if summed, the 60% total value of this assignment should be compared to the total available points across all modules which is 60%/800% or 7.5% of the total available grades for the year.

3 week first assignment deadline - All assignment deadlines were reviewed by the School Rep (Garry Clawson) and academic staff and approved by the Director of Teaching and Learning (Chris Headleand). With my participation I am able to then add comment and request changes where there may be bunching or clashing of assignments. This year we made 8 changes from the proposed assignment timetable and the reps wrote a full blog about this process which can be found here. The initial project time line was not one of the requested changes. However, this project lead time was specifically discussed. It was explained that this assignment lead time was due to experiences across previous years where the first assignment was not due until the 5th Dec. This caused significant issues as in several circumstances students had at that stage (end of Semester A) not yet fully clarified their projects or even met with their supervisor as they used the 1st assignment as a milestone for this initial discussion. Due to this it was considered, on the whole, better to have a tighter deadline to ensure that all students have full time to complete their project specifications and to support those who had not yet found something as early as possible in the academic year. This on the whole was thought to enable a much better project delivery in the 60% and final 20% sections, overall leading to improved marks for their project dissertation.
